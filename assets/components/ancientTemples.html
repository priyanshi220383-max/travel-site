Ancient Temples of Uttarakhand
⭐ 4.7 / 5 Tourist Rating

Uttarakhand's mountain valleys shelter some of India's most extraordinary ancient temples — hidden from mainstream tourism, protected by the Archaeological Survey of India, and alive with active worship dating back 1,200 years. The 9th-century Katarmal Sun Temple near Almora is only the second sun temple in India after Konark, its entrance door now in the National Museum Delhi. The Baijnath Shiva temple complex in Bageshwar district is a masterpiece of 12th-century Katyuri architecture. The Lakhudiyar rock paintings near Almora — ochre handprints and animals on a river cliff — predate the pyramids. Together these sites form an open-air museum of Himalayan civilisation spanning 6,000 years.

Featured Guides

Katarmal Sun Temple

India's second most important sun temple, dating to the 9th century AD. The ornate entrance door with its carved deities is now in the National Museum, Delhi. The 45 smaller shrines around the main temple are exquisite.

Baijnath Temple Complex

A cluster of 18 Shiva temples on the banks of the Gomti river in Bageshwar, dating to 1150 AD. The main Vaidyanath (healer Shiva) temple has a continuously burning lamp that pilgrims claim has not been extinguished in 800 years.

Lakhudiyar Rock Paintings

On a river cliff 25 km from Almora, ochre and white rock paintings show human figures, animals and geometric patterns dating to 5,000–6,000 BC — older than the pyramids of Egypt.

Dwarahat Temples

A forgotten cluster of 55 temples in Dwarahat town, representing four different architectural periods from the 10th–12th centuries. Rarely visited, remarkably preserved, deeply atmospheric.

Dunagiri Temple

A hilltop Vaishno Devi-form temple at 2,480 m above Dwarahat, reached by 365 steps. The temple is mentioned in the Mahabharata as the place where Draupadi prayed. Ancient and serenely beautiful.
